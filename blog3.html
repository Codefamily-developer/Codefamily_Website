Artificial Intelligence




Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a field of computer science dealing with the simulation of human intelligence—that is, the study and design of intelligent agents (systems that perceive their environment and act upon it). AI systems have been defined as any system that perceives its surroundings and takes actions based on those perceptions. Artificial intelligence research overlaps significantly with fields such as machine learning, robotics, natural language processing, cognitive psychology, philosophy, linguistics, neuroscience, evolutionary biology, computer vision, computational neuroscience, control theory, optimization theory, and many others. Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a branch of computer science dealing with the simulation of human thought processes. AI systems are designed to work and think just like humans. Artificial intelligence is a broad term that includes machine learning, natural language processing, expert systems, neural networks, cognitive architectures, autonomous agents, roboticists, and many others. Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a branch of computer science dealing with the simulation of human thought processes and intelligent behavior. AI research covers many different sub-fields including machine learning, natural language processing, planning, reasoning, knowledge representation, robotics, autonomous agents, neural networks, evolutionary computation, fuzzy logic, probabilistic inference, optimization, data mining, databases, theory of computation, and others. The field of artificial intelligence was founded by Alan Turing who proposed his testable algorithm for a machine capable of thinking in 1950. In 1956, John McCarthy organized the first international conference on Artificial Intelligence at Stanford University where he coined the term "artificial intelligence". In 1957, Dartmouth College professor Frank Rosenblatt published Perceptrons, the first book about artificial neurons and neural networks. He introduced the perceptron model, a mathematical function approximator inspired by the structure of the brain's neuron network. In 1959, Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert developed the AIMA (Artificial Intelligence Machines), a collection of interactive programming environments designed to teach students how to program computers. In 1962, Allen Newell and Herbert Simon presented their work on the Logic Theory of Computation, which laid out the basic principles of symbolic logic and formal languages. In 1965, Richard Hamming published his seminal paper on error correcting codes, which laid the foundation for modern coding theory. In 1966, Marvin Minsky and Edward Feigenbaum published their famous report on the state of AI, titled "Computational Capabilities of Symbolic Systems", which described the limitations of current technology and the potential future directions of AI research. In 1967, Stephen Wolfram published A New Kind of Science, which popularized cellular automata as models of computation and laid out the foundations of computational physics. In 1969, Marvin Minsky and Claude Shannon published their classic paper on universal computation, which laid out the theoretical basis for the connection between computability and physical systems. In 1971, John McCarthy organized the second International Joint Conference on Artificial Intelligence (IJCAI). In 1972, Marvin Minsky and Fernando Flores published their book on Connectionism, which laid out the basics of neural networks and introduced the idea of parallel distributed processing. In 1973, Marvin Minsky and Ray Solomonoff published their famous paper on Probabilistic Induction, which laid out the foundations of inductive inference and probability theory. In 1974, Marvin Minsky and Robert Gallager published their paper on the Minimum Description Length Principle, which laid the foundations of information compression and Kolmogorov complexity. In 1975, Allen Newell and Terry Winograd published their landmark paper on Understanding Natural Language, which laid the foundations for computational linguistics.
