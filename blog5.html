Natural Language Processing (NLP)




Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a field of computer science that focuses on developing algorithms that allow computers to understand human language. NLP is often used in conjunction with artificial intelligence, machine learning, and data mining. In addition, NLP is used in many applications including speech recognition, text-to-speech conversion, information extraction, question answering, summarization, sentiment analysis, and topic detection.
The goal of NLP is to create programs that can automatically analyze text and convert it into structured data. There are two types of NLP techniques: rule-based and statistical. Rule-based systems use predefined rules to identify patterns in text. Statistical methods rely on probability models to determine the likelihood of certain words appearing together.

Rule-Based Systems
A rule-based system uses a set of rules to identify patterns in the input text. These rules are then applied to the text to produce output. A simple example would be a program that identifies the names of people mentioned in a sentence. The algorithm might look at each word individually and compare them to a list of known names. If the name matches, the algorithm records the name.

Statistical Methods
Statistical methods are based on probability theory. Probability is the mathematical measure of uncertainty about whether something will happen. To calculate the probability of an event occurring, we need to know what the chances are of that event happening. We do this using statistics. Statistics is the study of how likely events are to occur.
There are three basic steps involved in statistical modeling: data collection, data preparation, and model building. Data collection involves gathering relevant facts about the problem. Data preparation involves cleaning the data and preparing it for analysis. Model building is where the actual analysis takes place.

Data Collection
In order to collect data, we first have to define our problem. What exactly are we trying to solve? Once we have defined the problem, we need to gather facts about it. Facts are pieces of information that help us answer questions. Facts may include things like the number of people who live in New York City, the average age of a person in the United States, or the percentage of people who own cats.

Data Preparation
Once we have collected facts, we need to clean them up. Cleaning up means removing any extraneous information that doesn't belong in the final analysis. For example, if we were analyzing the number of people who own cats, we wouldn't want to count pets. We'd only want to count people. Similarly, if we wanted to find out how old people are in the U.S., we wouldn't want to include children.

Model Building
After collecting and cleaning up the data, we're ready to build a model. A model is a simplified representation of reality. Models are useful because they make complex problems easier to understand. However, models aren't always perfect. Sometimes they miss details or oversimplify the truth. That's okay though, because we don't expect models to be 100% accurate. Instead, we use models to get a general idea of what's going on.
